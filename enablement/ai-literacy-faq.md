# AI Literacy FAQ — Common Lawyer Questions

Straight answers for skeptical professionals. Use these in workshops, lunch-and-learns, and one-on-ones.

---

## "Will this replace me?"

**No.** AI handles the first pass on high-volume, structured work — due diligence review, clause extraction, document summarization. It does not exercise legal judgment, build client relationships, argue in court, or develop case strategy. What it does is eliminate the 15 hours of reading boilerplate so you can spend your time on the 2 hours that actually require your expertise.

The risk isn't AI replacing lawyers. The risk is lawyers who use AI replacing lawyers who don't.

---

## "Is my client data safe?"

**In firm-approved tools: yes.** Harvey and the firm's internal AI tools operate under enterprise agreements with zero data retention (ZDR). Your prompt and the AI's response are purged from the model provider's servers immediately after generation. The data is never used to train the underlying models. The architecture is designed so Client A's data cannot appear in Client B's output.

**In consumer tools (ChatGPT, Claude personal, Gemini): no.** These tools may use your input for training. Pasting client data into consumer AI is a confidentiality breach. Don't do it.

---

## "How do I know it's not making things up?"

**You verify.** The AI provides its chain of reasoning with every output. It cites the specific clause, section, or passage that drove its conclusion. You read the cited source and confirm. If the AI flags a risk in section 8.3, you open section 8.3 and check.

The system is designed so you can see *why* it reached its conclusion — not just *what* it concluded. Black-box outputs are unacceptable for legal work.

That said: always verify citations. AI can fabricate case names and holdings that look real. Never cite an AI-generated case without pulling it from Westlaw or LexisNexis yourself.

---

## "What if the AI gets it wrong and I don't catch it?"

**The system is designed to surface uncertainty.** When the AI has low confidence in its analysis, it escalates to you explicitly rather than hiding behind false certainty. Red flags are mandatory review. Confidence below 70% triggers automatic escalation.

But ultimately: you are responsible for the output. The AI is a tool, not a lawyer. Your name is on the filing, not the model's.

## "How do I bill for AI-assisted work?"

**Under ABA Formal Opinion 512**, fees must be reasonable. If AI reduces a 15-hour task to 2 hours, you cannot bill the client for 15 hours at your standard rate.

For hourly matters: bill the actual time spent reviewing and finalizing the AI's output — not the time the AI saved.

For fixed-fee matters: the AI efficiency increases your margin. The firm prices the engagement based on the outcome, not the hours. This is where AI becomes a profitability driver.

The firm's tracking frameworks capture exactly how much time AI saved per matter, so the pricing team has the data they need.

---

## "Can clients opt out of AI on their matters?"

**Yes.** The firm's AI governance framework (published June 29, 2026) explicitly allows clients to request restrictions on AI use for substantive legal work. Foundation AI (embedded in standard productivity tools) generally cannot be disabled on a per-client basis.

## "I already use ChatGPT. Why should I switch?"

Three reasons:

1. **Confidentiality.** Consumer ChatGPT may use your input for training. Firm tools don't.
2. **Auditability.** Every interaction in firm tools is logged. If a client or regulator asks what AI did on a matter, the answer is one query away.
3. **Quality.** Harvey is built for legal work. It's grounded in LexisNexis, trained on legal reasoning patterns, and designed to produce outputs attorneys can rely on as first drafts.

The governed tools are better, safer, and faster for legal work than the consumer tools you're using now. That's by design.

## "How do I get started?"

1. **Attend a 45-minute workshop** — your practice group champion runs these monthly.
2. **Try one task** — pick a real, current matter. Run one prompt. See what comes back.
3. **Review the output** — spend 5 minutes checking the AI's work. You'll learn what it's good at and what it's not.
4. **Report what you find** — good or bad. Every piece of feedback makes the system better.
5. **Come to office hours** — drop in, ask questions, bring a document you want help with.
