# AI Adoption Playbook

How to get lawyers to actually use the tools. Not theory — operations.

---

## The Core Insight

Deploying software is 10% of the battle. The other 90% is human psychology.

You cannot mandate AI adoption by policy memo. Lawyers — especially partners who control $40M books of business — will simply opt out. You have to make the governed tools so good, so fast, and so embedded in daily workflow that the alternative feels like working without email.

---

## The Champion Network

### Why champions
A partner will listen to another partner before they listen to IT. A senior associate will follow another senior associate before they read a training guide. Peer influence is the adoption engine.

### How to build it
1. **Identify** 2-3 champions per practice group. Look for: respected by peers, technically curious (not necessarily technically skilled), frustrated with current inefficiencies, willing to be seen trying something new.
2. **Train** them first. Give them early access. Let them find the bugs before the broader rollout. Their feedback shapes the tool.
3. **Arm** them with: talking points for skeptical colleagues, a 5-minute demo they can do at a practice group meeting, answers to the 10 most common objections.
4. **Celebrate** them. Mention them in firm communications. Give them credit in innovation updates. Make being a champion a career positive.

### Champion profile (what to look for)
- Senior associate or junior partner — enough authority to influence, enough proximity to the work to use the tools daily
- Has complained about process inefficiency in the past year
- Respected for legal judgment, not just tech enthusiasm
- Willing to give honest feedback, not just cheerlead

## The Lunch-and-Learn Format

### Why it works
- Low commitment: 45 minutes, food provided
- Peer-led: a champion from their practice group, not an IT trainer
- Concrete: uses sanitized examples from their actual work, not generic demos
- Safe: questions are encouraged, skepticism is acknowledged, nobody is told they're wrong for being cautious

### Standard agenda
1. **5 min** — The champion: "Here's how I used this tool on a real matter last week, and here's what happened."
2. **15 min** — Live demo with a sanitized document from the practice group's work. Show the full cycle: upload → AI analysis → human review → output.
3. **15 min** — Hands-on: each attendee runs one prompt on a sample document relevant to their work.
4. **10 min** — Q&A. The champion handles the easy ones. A K&I team member handles the technical ones.

### Follow-up
- Email within 24 hours: link to the tool, quick-start guide, champion's contact info
- Office hours: 30-minute drop-in sessions twice a week for the first month
- Check-in at 30 days: who's using it, who's not, what's blocking adoption

## The Feedback Loop

### Why it matters
Adoption dies when feedback goes nowhere. If a lawyer reports a problem and never hears back, they stop reporting. If they see their feedback result in a fix, they become an advocate.

### How to run it
1. **Capture:** Every user interaction has a feedback mechanism (thumbs up/down, free-text, "report an issue").
2. **Acknowledge:** Within 48 hours, the reporter gets a personal response: "We saw your note about X. Here's what we're doing about it."
3. **Triage:** Bugs → technology team. Usability issues → K&I + technology. Feature requests → K&I backlog. Accuracy issues → quality review pipeline.
4. **Close the loop:** When the fix ships, tell the reporter. "The issue you flagged with the indemnification analysis is fixed. Here's what changed."
5. **Measure:** Feedback volume, resolution time, reporter satisfaction. If feedback dries up, adoption is either perfect (unlikely) or people gave up (more likely).

## The Objection Playbook

### "My work is too bespoke for AI."
**Response:** "The AI doesn't do your work. It does the first pass so you can focus on the bespoke part. If you spend 2 hours finding the relevant clauses in a 200-page document, the AI does that in 30 seconds. You still decide what they mean."

### "I don't trust it."
**Response:** "Good. You shouldn't trust it. That's why every AI output requires human review. The tool recommends. You decide. The audit trail proves you reviewed it."

### "It'll make mistakes and I'll get sanctioned."
**Response:** "The 700+ sanction cases involve lawyers who submitted AI output without review — usually from consumer tools like ChatGPT. Our tools are governed, auditable, and require human sign-off. The architecture prevents the nightmare scenario."

### "I don't have time to learn this."
**Response:** "The learning curve is 45 minutes. The time savings is 10-15 hours per month. At your billing rate, that's $12,000-$22,500 in recovered time — per month."

### "My clients won't want AI used on their matters."
**Response:** "85% of clients say firms should disclose AI use — they want transparency, not prohibition. Our client value reports show them exactly what AI did and what it saved. Most clients are impressed, not alarmed. And they can opt out if they want."

### "This is just a fad."
**Response:** "69% of lawyers already use AI individually. Kirkland & Ellis is investing $500M in proprietary AI. Harvey is valued at $11B. The question isn't whether AI transforms legal practice. It's whether we're leading that transformation or reacting to it."
